Welcome to the MIDS!
As you prepare to join the programme, please consult the Welcome Guide, which provides key information on administrative procedures, arrival planning, and student life in Geneva.  It covers visa and residence permit requirements, accommodation, health insurance, and practical aspects of settling in, and should be used to prepare ahead of the start of the academic year.

Accommodation
The rental market in Geneva is highly competitive, with demand exceeding supply. Securing suitable and affordable accommodation can be challenging, particularly if arrangements are made late. Admitted students are therefore responsible for securing accommodation and are encouraged to plan early.

All
admitted students are invited to complete the Accommodation Application Form to indicate their housing preferences and
interest in the available MIDS-arranged accommodation options.  Two main accommodation options are available:


IHEID student residences (Grand Morillon and Edgar & Danièle de Picciotto)   Applications must be submitted directly by you through the Graduate Institute Housing portal  Please select IHEID (and not UNIGE) when completing your application. This will ensure that your application is correctly
processed for the MIDS programme and allows it to be considered in
priority.


RUI residences (Rothschild, Carlton, La Voie Creuse)   Admitted
students interested in RUI accommodation will be contacted directly by RUI with further instructions in June 2026; applicants are therefore kindly asked not to contact the RUI residences directly.



Please note that accommodation is subject to availability.
Rooms are allocated on a first-come, first-served basis, and
preferences cannot be guaranteed. A no-pets policy applies to these student residences. Further details are provided in the housing emails.

Visa application for non EU/EFTA citizens This page section contains essential information for incoming MIDS students who are not citizens of an EU or EFTA country and therefore require a Swiss student visa to enter Switzerland. The visa process involves both Swiss federal and cantonal (Geneva) authorities and can be complex and time‑consuming. For this reason, early preparation is critical: the process may take up to three months, and you should begin as soon as possible once admitted.
The downloadable note below provides detailed guidance on how to apply for your visa, which documents to submit, where to find country‑specific requirements, and what to expect before travelling and upon arrival in Geneva. While we have taken care to ensure that the information is accurate at the time of publication, rules and procedures may change. You should therefore always consult the official Swiss authorities' websites linked below and follow their instructions closely. Please note that immigration procedures are an individual responsibility, and entering Switzerland on a tourist visa is not permitted for MIDS studies.

Preparing for your career journey at the MIDS
As you prepare to join the MIDS, we invite you to reflect early on the professional realities of international dispute settlement (IDS). This is a highly competitive field with varied career paths and timelines, shaped by factors such as prior experience, jurisdictional qualifications, language skills, and market conditions. There is no single "right" path - some students secure opportunities before the program begins, others during the year, and many after graduation. All of these trajectories are normal and well represented within the MIDS alumni community.
The MIDS equips students with a rigorous academic foundation, practical skills, and access to a truly global network of practitioners and alumni. At the same time, it is important to be clear about how careers in IDS develop. The MIDS is not a placement programme and does not place students into internships or jobs. Instead, our Career Services team works closely with each student to provide individualized guidance, training, feedback, and access to opportunities - helping students identify, pursue, and prepare for roles aligned with their goals. To support transparent and informed preparation, we share the Career Realities in International Dispute Settlement handout with all admitted students. This guide outlines what to expect in terms of recruitment timelines, application strategies, and the range of professional paths taken by MIDS alumni. We strongly encourage you to read it carefully and begin thinking early about how you would like to approach the year ahead. Your MIDS year is the foundation of a long-term career and understanding the landscape early will allow you to make the most of it.

Frequently Asked Questions
Can I work during the MIDS programme?
Due
to the programme's intensity and full-time schedule, students are generally not
advised to work during the academic year. However, some part-time research or internships
may be possible depending on visa status and workload. In addition, kindly note
that certain nationalities are not authorised to work in Switzerland with a
student visa.

Learn more

What kind of visa support is available for international students?
The MIDS provides guidance and documentation to
help admitted students obtain Swiss student visas. The admissions team supports
applicants throughout the process. Learn more  

Is housing provided or supported by the MIDS?
While
the MIDS does not offer on-campus housing, admitted students receive support in
finding accommodation in Geneva, including access to university housing
platforms and local resources.

Learn more  

Are visa and residence permit fees covered by MIDS-managed scholarships?
No. These fees are considered personal administrative
expenses and must be paid by the student, regardless of scholarship
status.

Can I do an internship in Europe as part of the MIDS programme?
Yes. You may
substitute one elective course with an internship (3-12 months) in Switzerland
or abroad, provided it relates to international dispute settlement. This
extends your student status and graduation date to September 2028.  In Geneva, with your student residence permit,
you will need to apply for work authorization from OCPM before starting. Non-EU
citizens must wait for approval before beginning the internship.

Can I stay in Switzerland after graduation to look for a job?
 Yes, through the
Neirynck initiative. You may apply for a six-month residence permit extension
to search for a job related to your MIDS degree. You must show financial means
and housing, and you're limited to working 15 hours/week during this period.
Your employer must still apply for a separate work permit, and Swiss
authorities may reject the request. The Neirynck extension is only for job
searching and cannot be prolonged.

Can I travel during the MIDS programme?
 Yes, short-term travel
is allowed between June and September 2026, but you must maintain your
residence in Geneva and return within six months (three months for Permit L
holders) to keep your residence permit valid.

Typical profile of a student who thrives in the MIDS programme
Students who excel at the MIDS tend to demonstrate:
Strong research, writing, and analytical skills, enabling them to engage deeply with complex legal materials.
A solid foundation in dispute settlement, allowing them to benefit fully from advanced coursework.
Intellectual curiosity and academic maturity, contributing to a dynamic learning environment.
Professionalism, resilience, and effective time management, essential for navigating the programme's intensity.
A proactive and realistic approach to career planning.
These characteristics are not strict requirements and do not
define the only path to success. The MIDS values diversity in academic
traditions, professional experiences, and personal backgrounds.

For most law firms, arbitral institutions, and organisations, applications for July 2027-July 2028 internships are not yet open and will appear later according to well‑established patterns: Law firms and arbitration boutiques Most international arbitration teams open applications between September 2026 and February 2027, often:

  • 9-12 months before the internship start date
  • In rolling waves, sometimes for short periods only

This applies to firms such as LALIVE, Schellenberg Wittmer, Three Crowns, Gaillard Banifatemi, White & Case, Clifford Chance, Debevoise, WilmerHale, Quinn Emanuel, Herbert Smith Freehills, Latham & Watkins, and similar firms across Europe, the UK, and beyond.

  • PCA (Permanent Court of Arbitration) internships are offered quarterly, with applications due approximately four months before the relevant term (for a July start, typically March of that year).

  • Other organisations (CMAP, ASA, professional associations, corporate legal departments) follow similar or even shorter timelines.

The most effective approach is to prepare early (research, CV drafting, tracking employers) without feeling pressure to apply everywhere immediately. The MIDS Career Services will share opportunities as they arise and help you interpret timing, expectations, and strategy throughout the year. Understanding the rhythm of recruitment now will allow you to approach the process calmly and realistically once applications begin to open.

title: >- Can I work during the MIDS programme? description: >-

Due to the programme's intensity and full-time schedule, students are generally not advised to work during the academic year. However, some part-time research or internships may be possible depending on visa status and workload. In addition, kindly note that certain nationalities are not authorised to work in Switzerland with a student visa. Learn more (external link)

- title: >- What kind of visa support is available for international students? description: >-

The MIDS provides guidance and documentation to help admitted students obtain Swiss student visas. The admissions team supports applicants throughout the process. Learn more (external link) 

- title: >- Is housing provided or supported by the MIDS? description: >-

While the MIDS does not offer on-campus housing, admitted students receive support in finding accommodation in Geneva, including access to university housing platforms and local resources. Learn more (external link) 

- title: >- Are visa and residence permit fees covered by MIDS-managed scholarships? description: >-

No. These fees are considered personal administrative expenses and must be paid by the student, regardless of scholarship status.

- title: >- Can I do an internship in Europe as part of the MIDS programme? description: >-

Yes. You may substitute one elective course with an internship (3-12 months) in Switzerland or abroad, provided it relates to international dispute settlement. This extends your student status and graduation date to September 2028.  In Geneva, with your student residence permit, you will need to apply for work authorization from OCPM before starting. Non-EU citizens must wait for approval before beginning the internship.

- title: >- Can I stay in Switzerland after graduation to look for a job? description: >-

 Yes, through the Neirynck initiative. You may apply for a six-month residence permit extension to search for a job related to your MIDS degree. You must show financial means and housing, and you're limited to working 15 hours/week during this period. Your employer must still apply for a separate work permit, and Swiss authorities may reject the request. The Neirynck extension is only for job searching and cannot be prolonged.

- title: >- Can I travel during the MIDS programme? description: >-

 Yes, short-term travel is allowed between June and September 2026, but you must maintain your residence in Geneva and return within six months (three months for Permit L holders) to keep your residence permit valid.

- kind: block template: chapter title: >- Typical profile of a student who thrives in the MIDS programme slug: >- typical-profile-of-a-student-who-thrives-in-the-mids-programme ranks: base: 2 self: 2 top: active: true title: value: >- Typical profile of a student who thrives in the MIDS programme heading: 2 data: layout: no_background text: >-

Students who excel at the MIDS tend to demonstrate:

  • Strong research, writing, and analytical skills, enabling them to engage deeply with complex legal materials.
  • A solid foundation in dispute settlement, allowing them to benefit fully from advanced coursework.
  • Intellectual curiosity and academic maturity, contributing to a dynamic learning environment.
  • Professionalism, resilience, and effective time management, essential for navigating the programme's intensity.
  • A proactive and realistic approach to career planning.

These characteristics are not strict requirements and do not define the only path to success. The MIDS values diversity in academic traditions, professional experiences, and personal backgrounds.

What MIDS graduates wish they had known before starting the program
Many graduates shared that the program is largely as advertised-and that their own research prepared them well. Still, a few things consistently came up:

"How hard and intense this program is."
"That we would wear formal clothes during classes."
"The huge amount of reading required before each class."

Below is a curated set of insights and advice from recent graduates to help incoming students make the most of their MIDS experience.

1. Academic Preparation

  • Begin the thesis early: "Start thinking about and writing the thesis ASAP."
  • Brush up on foundational topics: "Read up on Private International Law and basics of domestic arbitration law before joining."
  • Be ready to study intensively from day one: "Don't come if you are not ready to study extremely hard for an entire year."


2. Career planning

  • Start early-much earlier than you think: "Internship applications start early-often a year in advance." "Begin planning from day one."
  • The academic calendar matters: "The first semester is more loaded with grades and courses; the second focuses more on career opportunities."
  • Be proactive: "Hustle from the start."
  • Use your network: "Do not hesitate to network-it opens so many doors." "Your classmates will be one of your best resources."


3. Program intensity

  • Expect a demanding workload: "The program is rigorous and demanding, but rewarding."
  • It's challenging, but manageable and worth it: "The MIDS program is the pursuit of excellence." "It assures well‑rounded development and prepares you for the world."
  • But don't panic: "It's not as intense as some may depict it ;) Don't worry."
  • Pace yourself: "The MIDS year is a marathon, not a sprint."
  • Time management is crucial: "Plan your calendar early - deadlines cluster."
  • Avoid last‑minute studying: "Don't postpone readings. There is too much to cover and last‑minute reviews don't stick."
  • Stay on top of readings and note‑taking: "Read all of your assignments and take notes before class." "Consistent pre‑class preparation will help you get the most out of the program."


4. Well‑being & Mental resilience

  • The toughest months: "Stay mentally resilient during November and December; the pace eases later."
  • Geneva can feel isolating-plan for connection: "Make time for social activities and self‑care."


5. Engagement

  • Participate actively: "Engage with professors and peers-ask questions, attend office hours, collaborate."
  • Lean on your cohort: "Your classmates will be one of your best resources."
  • Trust yourself and your peers: "Go with the flow, trust the process, and trust your classmates. Everyone is benevolent."

  6. Practical tips for life in Geneva

  • Expect variable weather: "Pack for variable weather; Geneva winters can be tough."
  • Budget wisely: "Groceries are expensive: consider cross border shopping."
  • Travel expectations: "Don't expect to travel too much."
